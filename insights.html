Perspectives on Decision Infrastructure

Observations on how organizations form, govern, and preserve strategic decisions — and on the conditions under which that continuity begins to fail.

Institutional Memory

The quiet erosion of decision memory

Organizations accumulate decisions over years, but the reasoning behind those decisions erodes within months. The problem is structural: decisions are recorded as byproducts of other processes — buried in meeting minutes, project charters, and email threads. When people leave, the context leaves with them. What remains is the outcome, stripped of the assumptions, constraints, and trade-offs that produced it.

Systems Thinking

Treating the decision as a first-class object

Enterprise systems treat projects, risks, requirements, and workflows as first-class objects — entities with defined structure, state, and lifecycle. Decisions, despite being the authorizing act behind all of these, have no equivalent representation. Elevating the decision to a structured entity with components, governed states, and lineage introduces a new category of organizational capability.

Governance

What happens to governance after the meeting ends

Most governance processes focus on the moment of approval — a committee convenes, reviews material, and grants authorization. What happens afterward is largely ungoverned. The approved direction may drift during execution, the constraints accepted during approval may be forgotten, and the risk assessments may never be revisited. Governance that ends at the approval event is incomplete governance.

AI & Judgment

The role of AI in institutional judgment

In the context of enterprise decisions, AI serves a function distinct from prediction or recommendation. It assists in structuring and analyzing a decision — surfacing assumptions, identifying risk patterns, generating summaries. Its value lies in extending human judgment through structured analysis while preserving the fact that authority over the decision remains with people.

Audit & Evidence

Auditability that begins before execution

Audit processes typically examine execution records. Yet the most consequential audit question is often about the decision that preceded execution: what was the basis for the choice? If this question can only be answered through retrospective reconstruction, the audit is working with incomplete evidence. Auditability that begins at decision formation produces a fundamentally stronger record.

Continuity

Decision continuity across leadership transitions

When executives, program directors, or senior architects change roles, the decisions they made do not transfer cleanly. Successors inherit outcomes and active programs but rarely receive the full reasoning behind the strategic choices that shaped them. This discontinuity leads to redundant analysis, conflicting direction, and a weakened sense of institutional purpose.
