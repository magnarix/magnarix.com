Decisions that carry consequences are made every day.

They shape systems, direction, risk, and commitments.

But once they are made, they begin to disappear.

They do not vanish at once.
They fragment.

A portion remains in a document.
Another in a meeting.
Some in memory, held by individuals who move on.

What appears to persist is the outcome.
What does not is the reasoning that made it necessary.

Over time, organizations continue to act,
but no longer from the understanding that formed those actions.

Decision Structure

The decision as a structured object

In most organizations, a decision exists as a conclusion — a direction chosen, a resource allocated, a commitment made. The context that produced it — the constraints weighed, the alternatives considered, the risks acknowledged — disperses across systems, conversations, and individual memory.

In MagnaRix, the decision exists as a composed object. Each decision carries its objectives, constraints, assumptions, alternatives, trade-offs, risks, and approvals as structured, persistent elements. They are not annotations. They are the decision.

Objectives & Constraints

What the decision aims to achieve, and the boundaries within which it must operate. These define the decision space before any choice is made.

Assumptions & Alternatives

The conditions taken as given, and the paths that were considered. Preserving these prevents future teams from re-deriving what was already understood.

Risks & Approvals

What was acknowledged as uncertain, and who authorized the direction. These elements bind a decision to its governance context.

Decision Continuity

What persists when the people move on

Organizations do not lose decisions. They lose the understanding behind them.

Institutional memory

Every decision in MagnaRix persists as an immutable record. When leadership changes, teams reorganize, or programs evolve, the reasoning behind prior choices remains available. Not as a summary in a handover document — as the actual structured record, with its full context intact.

Lineage across time

Decisions relate to one another. An architectural choice constrains later technical decisions. A strategic commitment shapes program priorities. MagnaRix preserves these connections, so that later decisions can be understood in relation to the earlier ones that shaped them.

The end of reconstruction

When organizations need to understand why something was decided, they typically reconstruct the answer from fragments — interviewing people, searching documents, assembling a narrative after the fact. MagnaRix makes this reconstruction unnecessary. The record already exists, in the form it was created.

Replay & Understanding

Seeing what was known at the time

There is a difference between knowing what was decided and understanding the conditions under which it was decided. MagnaRix supports decision replay — the ability to reconstruct the full state of a decision at any point in its lifecycle.

What was known. What was assumed. What risks were accepted. Who reviewed, who approved, and what the decision looked like at the moment of authorization. This is the kind of understanding that governance requires and that most organizations cannot produce without significant effort.
