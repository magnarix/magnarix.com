Perspectives on Decision Infrastructure

Observations and analysis on how organizations form, govern, and preserve strategic decisions — and where current approaches fall short.

Decision Memory

Why Enterprises Lose Decision Memory

Organizations accumulate decisions over years, but the reasoning behind those decisions erodes within months. The problem is structural: decisions are recorded as byproducts of other processes — buried in meeting minutes, project charters, and email threads. When people leave, the context leaves with them. What remains is the outcome, stripped of the assumptions, constraints, and trade-offs that produced it. This creates a form of institutional amnesia that compounds over time, forcing teams to re-derive context that was once explicitly understood.

Enterprise Architecture

The Decision as a First-Class Object

Enterprise systems treat projects, risks, requirements, and workflows as first-class objects — entities with defined structure, state, and lifecycle. Decisions, despite being the authorizing act behind all of these, have no equivalent representation. They exist as unstructured text in systems designed for other purposes. Elevating the decision to a first-class object means giving it defined components, governed states, ownership, and lineage — making it queryable, auditable, and persistent in ways that current approaches cannot achieve.

Governance

Governance After the Meeting Ends

Most governance processes focus on the moment of approval — a committee convenes, reviews material, and grants authorization. What happens to that decision afterward is largely ungoverned. The approved direction may drift during execution, the constraints accepted during approval may be forgotten, and the risk assessments may never be revisited. Effective governance extends beyond the approval event. It requires a persistent record that connects the authorized decision to its execution, enabling ongoing alignment and accountability.

AI & Judgment

AI and Institutional Judgment

AI is increasingly used to generate recommendations and predictions. In the context of enterprise decisions, AI serves a different function: it assists in structuring and analyzing a decision, not in making it. The distinction matters. AI can surface assumptions that decision-makers may have overlooked, identify risk patterns across historical decisions, and generate structured summaries of complex contexts. The value is in augmenting human judgment with structured analysis — preserving the principle that authority over the decision remains with people.

Auditability

Why Auditability Begins Before Execution

Audit processes typically examine execution records — logs, approvals, change records. Yet the most consequential audit question is often about the decision that preceded execution: what was the basis for the choice? Who authorized it? What risks were acknowledged? If these questions can only be answered through retrospective reconstruction, the audit is working with incomplete information. Auditability that begins at decision formation — capturing reasoning, governance, and risk assessment in real time — produces a fundamentally stronger evidence base.

Continuity

Decision Continuity Across Leadership Transitions

Leadership transitions are a recurring feature of enterprise life. When executives, program directors, or senior architects change roles, the decisions they made do not transfer cleanly. Successors inherit outcomes and active programs but rarely receive the full reasoning behind the strategic choices that shaped them. This discontinuity leads to redundant analysis, conflicting direction, and a weakened sense of institutional purpose. Decision continuity requires records that persist beyond the tenure of any individual decision-maker.
